PROJECT OVERVIEW

Activo is a project that was created during my time as a Design student with Bloc. I had full creative freedom to execute my design process on this passion project. Activo is an application built for travelers. It solves the problem of not being able to have a culturally immersive experience while traveling, or simply put, "travel like a local." As traveling becomes more accessible and widespread, users expectations for their travel experiences change, and this is a problem that I have identified as a consequence of these changing circumstances. Being a passion project, I had a lot of fun developing this idea into a working prototype. DISCOVERY   

PROBLEM

After discovery was completed, the following problem was unveiled:

(1) travelers want a local perspective and experience but don't know any locals
(2) meeting strangers in a foreign city is difficult, and can be awkward

From this feedback the problem was identified: travelers need a tool to help connect them with locals, that also bridges the gap of uncomfortability in meeting a stranger.

SOLUTION

An application that allows travelers to find local people that advertize activities, so that the traveler can get a local experience, while also reducing social uncomfortability of meeting a new person by setting the expectation of sharing an activity together. This solution offers the user an experience that offers the user a better understand local life, and create a meaningful memory and relationship while traveling.

RESEARCH  

RESEARCH OVERVIEW

First off, I deployed a google forms survey through email, slack, reddit, and a community college student forum in Seattle, WA. I had a total of n=49 participants, though a majority of my respondents were international college students. This skewed the data somewhat, but for the purposes of this case study, there was a solid foundation of feedback to work with.

The initial problem hypothesis I was testing for was this: "learning language with a person while sharing an activity produces higher rates of comprehension than classroom learning." Survey responses showed that my hypothesis was confirmed, but the written responses showed that the activity based language meet up application wasn't what these users were looking for.

Responses included:

“I would want to do one of those activities, but I would never make a lesson plan centered around an activity. It's boring and tedious to come up with content for practicing language, but if there is an activity/goal involved and no pressure involved, it's easier to interact and I am more motivated to learn the vocabulary and try to speak."

"I don't have enough time to do this regularly"

"Teaching language is boring, I would hang out with someone while learning thought"

"I'd rather just cook together than feel pressured"

"Skipping the language lesson and just hanging out with the person seems better"

Survey Findings
  • of users living in a foreign country were interested in local culture.
  • were interested in trading skills with a local to make friends in the community.
  • said it is hard to make good friends with locals or become a part of the local community as a non-native language speaker

SURVEY TAKEAWAYS

Users don't want an activity language application, but survey responses show they are interested in an immersive cultural experience and participating in an activity. After interviewing three survey participants, the participants stated that they were very interested in learning more about whatever culture they were visiting, but were afraid of approaching strangers in a new city.

From the above survey results, we can see that users are interested in local culture, interested in trading skills to make friends, and found it hard to make friends with locals.

BRANDING  

DEVELOPING AN IDENTITY

At this point, I had a good understanding of what the user I was designing for was looking for in a product. Overall, the brand needed to illustrate something, modern, easy to use, and something that make users excited to expand their opportunities to meet new people abroad and have new experiences with interesting people.

Name

I had at least 5 different working sessions, attempting to come up with the perfect name for this application. I referred to this app as “skillshare” before it had a name, so I knew it needed to have something to do with collaboration, memory making, and doing an activity. Activo, a play on “activity” clearly is associated with the concept of the app, and it rolls off the tongue, so it stuck.

USER TESTING  

USABILITY TESTING OVERVIEW

Methods: (1)In Person Usability Testing, (2)Remote Usability Testing, (3)Remote A/B or Preference Testing

I conducted one round of user testing, as well as a preference test. I conducted user tests with four participants, two in person tests and two via Skype. I wrote a user test script which I used with each participant to ensure my tests and results were as consistent and objective as possible. While conducting these tests I focused on how the user executed the tasks, their verbal responses, as well as nonverbal responses in order to gain as much feedback as possible to maximize improvements on my product.

Sign in/up Screens Testing Results

Before

After

Results:

  1. user two stated that having the two feature colors side by side is distracting, and their eye was not gudied towards one action or the other.

  2. user one first thought that both buttons were "sign in" buttons.

  3. user one also stated that the color palette contrasted with the background image was not pleasing to the eye.

Solution:

  1. A new background image was created with illustrations also used in the onboarding screens to create a sense of cohesive branding for the user. The illustration colors were also chosen to compliment Activo's branding.

  2. Assuming that most users that see this screen would be new users, I made that the central call to action, with a clearly differentiated option for returning users. Differentiating these two actions reduce the cognitive load of choosing to sign up or log in, so ideally the user can read one button and just assume the other option at a glance.

Dashboard Testing Results

Results:

  1. user one stated the dual screen as unnecessary because they wouldn't be switching between traveler and host mode at home.

  2. user two stated they were confused about the purpose and motive behind the toggle switch.

  3. user two also stated that they are opposed to having the switch be in the account settings because they thought they would miss this feature altogether, or forget about it.

Solution:

  1. eliminate the ability to switch between screens because user doesn't understand the purpose of the dual screen.

  2. give users access to both traveler and host features 100% of the time because this eliminates confusion, and allows users easy access to both features of the application. This improves their experience because it allows user full access to features without having to make a decision about what best suits them. This eliminates confusion surrounding decision, and keeps the features accessible.

Date Selection Testing Results

Before

After

Results:

  1. user one got lost in the process of picking dates

  2. user two complained that there were too many steps for these simple tasks

Solution:

  1. created modal screen so user is no longer taken to a completely different screen to choose dates in order to cut down time spent on task to improve fluidness of the experience. Using a modal screen is also easier on the users cognitive load, as they don't have to interpret an entire new screen.

  2. shortened length of entire process from 6 selections to 3 selections to maximize the efficency of the process in order to keep users attention, and help them quickly move on to the next task.

Inbox & Messaging Testing Results

Before

After

Results:

  1. user one stated that it was confusing to switch between traveler and host mode for messages, which would cause messages to be overlooked or forgotten about.

  2. user attempted to leave message screen to find other message. In this specific case, to decrease confusion, the back arrow should only exist when viewing the actual message itself.

Solution:

  1. navigation in and out of the message screen is done with the main navigation bar, this clear label shows pertinent screen location information and signals that if a user wants to leave their inbox they should use the main navigation.

  2. one inbox screen was created to reduce decision making, and color coding was created to differentiate "host" messages from "traveler" messages.

Search Screen Testing Results

Before

After

Results:

  1. user one stated the two buttons were distracting, because they were both such bright colors.

  2. user one pointed out it wasn't clear the add to my trips button didn't take them to another page, and overlooked the "save trip" call to action completely.

  3. search button was resized to match all other call to action buttons to enhance asthetic continuity and make experience less distracting.

PREFERENCE TEST 1: SEARCH SCREEN

I conducted two preference tests, and had a total of n=24 participants. I acknowledge that ideally I would like to recruit more than 24 participants, but for the purpose of this case study I moved forward in making changes based on these results. I chose to conduct a preference test to test my own assumption that design A was more visually pleasing than design B. To my surprise that was not the case, users found that the grey "add activity" buttons were less of a cognitive load than the purple, and that the design was overall more cohesive and understandable.

Design A: 40%

Design B: 60%

PREFERENCE TEST 2: "SAVE TRIP" LOCATION

I conducted a preference test to see if users preferred having the "save trip" check box as part of the search criteria, or after the search button following the common "remember me" UI pattern. This preference test was conducted after the user testing was completed. Ideally, this question would be tested in another round of user testing, but due to time constraints, a preference test was administered in order to decide which button placement was most usable. Design A was selected, and users stated that having the "save" button would be overlooked if it took place before the search.

Design A: 72%

Design B: 28%
